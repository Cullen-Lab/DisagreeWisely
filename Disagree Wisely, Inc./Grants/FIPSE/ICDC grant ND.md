# 1\. Narrative

## **Integrating Civil Discourse into the Curriculum at Public, Private, Community and Historically Minority-Serving Colleges and Universities**

*This initiative introduces a fundamentally new model for strengthening civil discourse in higher education by treating sound reasoning and civil dialogue not as separate pedagogical goals, but as interdependent cognitive skills that must be taught together.*

Integrating Civil Discourse into the Curriculum (ICDC) introduces a fundamentally new model for strengthening civil discourse in higher education by treating sound reasoning and civil dialogue not as separate pedagogical goals, but as **interdependent cognitive skills that must be taught together**. Existing discourse programs typically focus on encouraging students to talk across differences, but they rarely ensure that students have the analytical tools needed to generate well-reasoned claims before entering conversation. Likewise, most critical-thinking curricula teach logic and argument analysis in isolation, without giving students structured opportunities to practice applying those skills in live dialogue. ICDC’s innovation is to **link these two domains—rigorous argument analysis and guided civil dialogue—through validated pedagogies and AI-assisted tools**, enabling students to form better arguments and then test those arguments responsibly in structured conversations. This integrated design matters because it directly addresses the root causes of polarization: not just incivility, but the widespread inability to distinguish sound from unsound reasoning and to revise one’s views in light of evidence.  
After establishing this integrated model, ICDC sponsors large-scale adoption. With support from FIPSE, ICDC will implement this integrated approach on 125 campuses—including R1 universities, HBCUs, community colleges, and civic institutes—reaching tens of thousands of students. By forming teams of Civil Discourse Fellows on each campus, we prompt a nationwide, faculty-led movement to reform the General Education Requirements to include a sound-reasoning-plus-dialogue pedagogy. The broad institutional diversity of our coalition, and the large numbers of students we reach initially, will allow the initiative to evaluate and refine the model while stimulating a cascading adoption that will transform civic education in the United States of America.

1. ## **Significance**

1. ## **Problem Statement**

In recent years, a rise in protest tactics has disrupted dialogue on U.S. college campuses. Controversial topics are discussed in an atmosphere of perceived–or real–violence. To address these breakdowns in civil discourse, administrators have tried various solutions. Committees have been formed to discuss the problem. Lectures have been sponsored on contentious issues. New faculty positions have been funded to pursue research on aligned topics. These are all good-faith attempts to address the problem. The most successful responses seem to have been Civic Institutes created to teach civic education. Those Centers take direct aim at the two most pressing issues: the lack of university-wide training in critical thinking and the absence of opportunities for all students to practice speaking respectfully with those with whom they disagree. The current challenge facing the Centers is to integrate their efforts with other academic programs to form coalitions of faculty capable of teaching civil discourse at scale. Since faculty control the General Education Requirements (GER), ICDC builds a large coalition of faculty with the vision–and tools–they need to reform it.  
	ICDC is an ambitious, research-based, national initiative that takes aim squarely at the problem. We will bring together three Civic Institutes, five universities, and three nonprofits to reach 95,064 students total, with 71,298 students trained in civil discourse groups and 23,766 in control. First, since we believe students must have something to say before they can be taught how to say it, we will teach them to think critically. Then, since we believe that students must be closely guided in real-time when they speak to opponents, we will use an AI-assisted agent to help them practice the rhetorical art of speaking charitably when in conversation with someone with whom they disagree. Research shows that large cohorts of students can be taught to understand and skillfully evaluate arguments by relying on an exciting new AI agent, Sway, that makes real-time interventions in disagreements and thereby teaches respectful dialogue.  
Our initiative is more likely to succeed in improving civil discourse and campus atmosphere than any other method for one reason; we do not aim to provide students with more information. We aim to develop their character. Civil discourse instruction is most successful when clearly focused on forming the student’s *habits of mind*. Our central objective is to cultivate in students two intellectual virtues: the confidence to *think for oneself*, and epistemic *humility about what one knows and does not know*.

### **1\. Insufficient confidence in one’s reasoning ability.** Many undergraduates—especially those without strong pre-college preparation—lack the confidence to evaluate arguments constructively in dialogue about complex social issues. 2. ## **Project Design**

1. **Innovative Tools for Critical Thinking Instruction**

To speak in a civil way one must know how to reason in a fair way. To provide this instruction, ICDC proposes to assist faculty Fellows as they integrate into their curricula an innovative combination of two existing nonprofit educational tools: thinkARGUMENTS and Sway. We selected thinkARGUMENTS because it uses two powerful pedagogical techniques, Mastery Learning and Argument Mapping, proven to improve student performance taken online [(Dwyer et al., 2012\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?dYIbKg). We have negotiated a special rate with ThinkerAnalytix of $10 per license, a far lower price than its main two competitors, Rationale ($30) and The Economist Education course, Critical Thinking ($920).   
To the extent of our knowledge, Sway is the only AI tool that reliably and immediately guides real-time conversations between two users who have established differences of opinion on the ethical question they are discussing. These two tools will provide instructors with the ability to teach reasoning skills with very little effort on their part because both tools can be assigned, used, and graded with minimal investment of an instructor’s time. ICDC will not only urge adoption of these two tools; it will also improve them by adding new functionalities for customization to each one. We will simultaneously study the impact that the improvements bring for student learning.

1. **thinkARGUMENTS (TA)**

thinkARGUMENTS is an online asynchronous course. It is centered on a research-backed framework called argument mapping for teaching students to analyze, visualize, and evaluate arguments with clarity, rigor, and intellectual humility, skills that sit at the heart of healthy civic discourse. Validated by external, independent studies described below, thinkARGUMENTS guides students through practice exercises and assessments with automatic feedback until they demonstrate a high level of proficiency on a set of reasoning skills (approximately 35 total skills) using a Mastery Learning model. The course gives large student cohorts a shared vocabulary and toolkit to navigate disagreement constructively, recognize strong and weak reasoning, and engage across differences. Built for use in any discipline, the program teaches the thinking skills students need to reason through discourse and disagreement.

2. **Sway**

Sway is a civil discourse practice community, an AI-supported platform proven to teach respectful dialogue about controversial issues. It pairs each student with a partner, the algorithm selecting students who disagree with each other on key ethical issues. The two then are prompted, guided, and supervised by a research-tested AI agent as they learn to mediate their differences in a respectful way. Sway’s AI-agent structures conversations which occur across disagreement. Because it generates prompts, interpretations, and suggestions during live discussions, Sway is an ideal test case for FIPSE priorities. Sway, produced by Disagree Wisely, has received significant external validation. In 2024, over 1,000 projects competed in the Learning Agency Tools Competition, and Sway won in the Post-Secondary Learning track. The Tools Competition is the world's largest edtech competition—a global program that has awarded over $20 million to 150+ innovators across 48 countries since 2020, reaching nearly 50 million learners and educators worldwide. Selection by this extraordinarily rigorous international review validates Sway's promise as a breakthrough approach to civil discourse education. ICDC will advance Sway’s development by underwriting a new feature, ChatMaps, a real-time argument visualization feature that will create a direct pedagogical bridge between thinkARGUMENTS’s asynchronous instruction and Sway's synchronous dialogue practice.

3. **TA \+ Sway**

thinkARGUMENTS and Sway bring three critical resources to ICDC: (1) technology to conduct basic research evaluating our methods, (2) capacity to accommodate large numbers as ICDC urges adoption of our methods, and (3) mechanisms to support assessment of each student’s gains in truthfulness, metareasoning, and consistency in fraught dialogues.  
	The thinkARGUMENTS course complements Sway’s work by functioning as a pedagogical tool to teach argument analysis. thinkARGUMENTS teaches students to decompose arguments, identify assumptions, generate counterarguments, and trace factual claims to their sources. We will assess thinkARGUMENTS’s ability to improve Sway conversations. We are particularly interested to learn whether we can improve reasoning “in the wild” using these two online resources, and how each resource can be improved on the basis of the data collected. We are interested in measuring gains in clarity, accuracy, open-mindedness, and belief-updating. To the extent of our knowledge, ICDC will be the largest of its kind to generate empirical evidence and new datasets about the ability of AI systems to teach civil discourse in the presence of disagreement. 

4. **Building on Similar or Related Efforts to Improve Relevant Outcomes**

ICDC integrates with and builds on the civil discourse and democratic leadership work of Civic Institutes, thinkARGUMENTS’s Mastery Learning pedagogy, and Sway’s AI civil discourse platform. These efforts have established records and proven outcomes; ICDC will amplify each of them by bringing the various groups together into one cohesive whole.  
	In terms of established usage, thinkARGUMENTS has been used at over 70 colleges, universities, and secondary schools in the last two years, with over 40,000 users on the platform since its initial launch in fall 2020\. Its retention rate among faculty and administrators assigning thinkARGUMENTS to their students is 93%. Sway, launched in 2024, has been used at over 80 colleges and universities by over 150 instructors, reaching approximately 8,000 students. Evaluations from users and instructors are very strong. Of the first 250 students to use Sway for course assignments, over 80% rated their chats “Awesome” or “Good.” Only 4% gave a negative rating–even though 94% of chats were about polarizing political, social, or moral issues. Over 90% of students said their partner was respectful and only 2% said they were offended by their partner’s opposing viewpoint. Nearly all students (90%) report feeling comfortable sharing their sincere views on the platform, 95% report feeling respected by their chat partner, and 80% say it was valuable to chat with someone who didn’t share their perspective.   
	The project also integrates with existing efforts on the campuses of the partner institutions. The partners were carefully selected to include a wide range of the basic types of higher education institutions. The University of Notre Dame, the lead institution, and Harvard are large private universities. The University of Texas-Austin is a large flagship university. North Carolina State University is a large Land-Grant. Camden County College is a two-year community. Edward Waters is a Historically Minority-Serving University. Each campus has existing programs which ICDC will build on and boost. In addition, ICDC will strengthen efforts at 125 additional campuses through the Civil Discourse Fellows Workshop.  
	ICDC takes advantage, too, of on-going research efforts. At North Carolina State University, the National Critical Thinking Initiative, supported by an ongoing $14,000 grant from the NC State College of Humanities & Social Sciences trains faculty from NC State, other U.S. universities, the Philippines, and Vietnam to integrate thinkARGUMENTS into their courses. A second initiative at North Carolina State, thinkPACK, is, according to the North Carolina State University Foundation, a “bold investment in the Pack’s Power to Think Critically,” which the Foundation supports with an ongoing $49,800 grant. The project is led by Alumni Association Distinguished Undergraduate Professor Gary Comstock, a philosopher, and Professor Daniel Gruehn, a developmental psychologist. Together they teach and empirically evaluate thinkARGUMENTS’s effectiveness when taught asynchronously online to hundreds of students.  
	Comstock’s and Gruehn’s research suggests thinkARGUMENTS is very effective. More than 680 entering students at NC State completed a test of critical thinking–the Critical Reasoning and Inference Test (CRIT). Half of them then completed thinkARGUMENTS. Preliminary findings show thinkARGUMENTS improved critical thinking scores by almost 10%, nearly a full letter grade over students who did not take thinkARGUMENTS. Students who completed thinkARGUMENTS plus an in-person 3-credit critical thinking course outperformed controls by 22%, more than a two letter grade advance \[F(4,612) \= 11.2, p\<.001, 𝜂2=.068\]. These are significant achievements in an area where it is very difficult to move the needle.  
	An independent study conducted at the University of Notre Dame found similar promising results (Blaschko & Kestigian, forthcoming). Faculty integrated thinkARGUMENTS into *God and the Good Life,* a popular large lecture course. The goals of this intervention were to increase the course’s intellectual rigor and improve students’ critical thinking skills. Use of the platform resulted in significant improvements, with average critical thinking pre- to post-test growth of 16% and 12% across two sections. Iterative changes to the framing of the platform inside the course led to increased student satisfaction while maintaining gains in critical thinking. This offers a replicable model for improving critical thinking instruction in large introductory and General Education courses included in this proposed project.

2. **Quality Controls & Continuous Improvement Efforts**

Extensive quality controls and milestones for continuous improvement are built into each of the four years of the initiative. Faculty leaders at the five lead institutions will maintain implementation logs documenting each team’s background, fidelity of adoption, and year-over-year persistence. These logs allow us to track uptake across the broader network of 125 institutions and to identify early challenges to be met and exemplary practices to be shared.  
	We will repeatedly measure student learning using thinkARGUMENTS mastery checks, the Critical Reasoning and Inference Test (CRIT), and analysis of Sway dialogues. These tools will allow us to assess gains in reasoning proficiency, intellectual humility, confidence, perspective-taking, and civil-discourse behaviors. To assess student performance we will use CRIT, a measure of each student's comprehension of ethical arguments [(Comstock & Gruehn, 2025\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?7Ihh0e). Developed and refined at North Carolina State by Profs. Comstock and Gruehn, CRIT comes in three versions.  Each version presents a different 1200-word argumentative essay about a controversial topic and 20 multiple-choice questions about the essay’s reasons, conclusions, objections, and rebuttals. Each of the three CRIT versions show good reliability and validity.  Scores on the test correlate strongly with logical reasoning skills and students’ grade point averages, and correlate, too, with dispositional critical thinking measures such as critical openness, reflective skepticism, and self-reported argumentation skills. Unlike most critical thinking tests, which are commercial–and prohibitively expensive for a large scale study–CRIT is publicly available for non-commercial purposes on the project page of the Open Science Foundation. We will analyze CRIT gain scores annually, while thinkARGUMENTS and Sway data provide complementary evidence about students’ argument structure, assumptions, and responsiveness to counterarguments.  
	Each team of three Fellows will develop a campus-specific implementation plan during their workshop. The Core Faculty will review and provide feedback on instructional best practices and research requirements. During their first year of implementation, each Fellow will submit mid-year and end-of-year progress reports documenting student participation, instructional methods, and any barriers encountered. Faculty leaders and subcontractors will conduct periodic virtual check-ins with campus teams to ensure fidelity, provide troubleshooting support, and identify needed improvements. Faculty leaders will use data from Fellow surveys, focus groups, and mid-year check-ins to revise workshops, refine onboarding materials, and recommend improvements to the thinkARGUMENTS and Sway platforms. Each new cohort benefits from adjustments made in prior years, ensuring that implementation strengthens over the life of the initiative.  
	Both ThinkerAnalytix and Disagree Wisely will follow quality-assurance procedures. ThinkerAnalytix will conduct internal testing and refinement of AI features developed under the project subcontract, followed by controlled pilot testing with Fellows to verify accuracy, clarity, and alignment with learning goals. User feedback (faculty and students) will be collected through surveys, in-platform chat tools, and structured focus groups. Each of the four development phases in the thinkARGUMENTS subcontract outlined below includes refinement based on this data.  
	Sway provides an additional layer of automated analytic quality control. For each dialogue, it generates machine-coded measures of clarity, fairness, and belief updating that can be summarized with appropriate statistics. These metrics quantify both improvement and uncertainty and can be computed across tens of thousands of conversations, giving the evaluator sensitive, scalable insight into student progress. Together, these human- and machine-generated data create a robust system of continuous improvement and repeated testing aligned with the project’s goals.

6. **Subawards & Consultation**  
   1. **ThinkerAnalytix \- Foundations of American Democracy: A Customization Subgrant**

The most important place to embed ICDC instructional practices is in the General Education Requirements (GER) of each institution. In this subcontract, thinkARGUMENTS (TA) will gain new AI-assisted functionality to allow faculty teaching a variety of GER courses to integrate our new model into their offerings. Existing thinkARGUMENTS modules focus on the skills needed to read and dissect short arguments of roughly 300 words or fewer. To further develop students’ civic skills, ThinkerAnalytix will develop a model course focused on the skills needed to read longer, argumentative, essays. The course, “Foundations of American Democracy,” (FAD) will be customizable to accommodate any instructor’s preferences in the selection of the classic American texts they assign. The TA infrastructure will automatically generate practice exercises and quiz questions over whatever texts the instructor assigns. Whether it is the Declaration of Independence, U.S. Constitution, Bill of Rights, The Federalist Papers, Letter from Birmingham Jail, Common Sense, or The Souls of Black Folks, FAD will guide students to learn to read these documents *as arguments*. For an example of a lesson, see the Sample Lesson in Other Attachments. TA will work with the personnel at NC State University in Years 1 and 2 to build, test, and refine the course. To support the technology development needed for this course, ICDC provides needed funding.  
The result will not simply be a new course. It will be a powerful template instructors in any field can use to assign bespoke reasoning exercises. As educators refine and share their AI-generated question banks across institutions, the system will grow into a national, community-driven resource supporting the broad integration of reasoning and civil discourse education across higher education. The new tools will enable instructors to show students how the thinkARGUMENTS cross-disciplinary framework for critical thinking applies in the context of the foundational historical texts, civic discourse, and disciplinary topics. On the student’s side, the work they do on the platform will have clear relevance to their specific coursework and academic pathways, thus broadening and deepening the critical thinking skills needed for civic and social life. 

2. **Disagree Wisely Consultation- Civil Discourse and Online Community-Building**

Disagree Wisely will create an AI-assisted online community where students will be introduced to students with views similar to theirs and, equally important, to students with an opposing view. Sway is a chat platform that connects students with differing perspectives into one-on-one conversations and facilitates better discussions between them. Developed with nonprofit partner Heterodox Academy and inspired by John Stuart Mill's radical view that engaging with opposing perspectives is an essential tool for improving reasoning and solving complex problems, Sway aims to create online spaces where we can all learn to discuss controversial issues more openly and constructively.  
	We will evaluate Sway’s ability to adhere to principles such as truthfulness (Does Sway accurately capture the user’s reasoning? Does it avoid hallucinations?), metareasoning (To what extent does it help users monitor their own reasoning, recognizing pressure points and implicit assumptions?), and consistency (Does it apply similar reasoning patterns across similar cases?). Our research will investigate when and why Sway displays or fails to display these principles. On the basis of the datasets we create we will be able to improve Sway’s capacity to reliably and productively adhere to the principles.

7. **Longitudinal Study Design**

Participating teams on 125 campuses will arrange for their institutions to contribute to a rigorous three-year longitudinal study. A team of three faculty members from each institution– with a preference for established teachers of critical thinking–will be selected as Civil Discourse Fellows. They will participate together in one of the three summer workshops. The following academic year they will assume joint responsibility for identifying, supervising, and teaching the three treatment groups. Students in the control will take the pre-, mid-, and post-tests but not thinkARGUMENTS or Sway. Controls will be students unaffiliated with the study drawn from classes in, for example, Psychology or History. Fellows will help enroll unaffiliated students in the control (by, e.g., speaking with their colleagues and coordinating the study with the Dean’s office). For design purposes, and to keep the treatments separated, the unaffiliated students in the control should not come from classes taught by the Fellow. While licenses for thinkARGUMENTS and Sway will be needed for the treatment groups, they will not be needed for the control.   
To maximize spread, we will finalize plans for the Institutes in Year 1\. The first Institute will occur in Year 2, with 33 teams of three Fellows, for a total of roughly 100 Fellows. In Year 3 we accept roughly 125 Fellows, and 150 in Year 4\. To defray workshop costs, we budget for 100 Fellows each year to travel to the workshop, with 25 Fellows in Year 3 and 50 Fellows in Year 4 attending virtually. Each Fellow agrees to four tasks:

1. Integrate thinkARGUMENTS into a course(s) to reach 34 students per year; integrate Sway into a second class of 34 students; and integrate thinkARGUMENTS \+ Sway into a third course of 34 students.  
2. Administer CRIT to all students as a pre-test and post-test at the beginning and end of the term  
3. Identify colleagues not affiliated with this study (e.g., in a Psychology class) who will give course credit for 34 students in a control.   
4. Supervise the issuing of a call for all students in the study at their institution to complete CRIT. To motivate participation, we will pay students in the control group $15 for completion of this post-test. Completion cannot occur before the semester they graduate, or the end of the grant period, whichever comes first.

These four quasi-randomized treatments occur in a staggered, non-randomized rollout across institutions, enabling estimation of causal effects via difference-in-differences, multilevel mixed-effects modeling, and sensitivity analyses for non-equivalence. The design produces hard evidence about the effectiveness of the four treatments by running institution-level moderation tests, faculty-level fidelity modeling, and student-level growth-curve estimation.

4. **Student Learning Evaluation Tools \- CRIT, TA, and Sway Data**

To evaluate student progress in each of the four treatments we will rely, but only in part, on student self-reports. Self-reports are an important source of information, but they are not sufficient. Reliable measures of what a student has actually learned require pre- and post-tests assessing progress. For this measure, we turn to CRIT, a validated open source instrument that determines a student’s ability to engage in the dialectical reasoning necessary for reaching one’s own conclusions about controversial issues. We administer CRIT at three points over the course of the grant tenure:  the pre-test when students enter the study, a mid-test at the end of the semester in which they enter, and a post-test during their last semester before graduation or the last semester of this initiative, whichever comes first. In this way we study student progress in civil discourse over a longer period than most other studies are able to do. And we measure it using more objective instruments than self-reports or Likert scales.  
Data collected through CRIT and online platforms for each student cohort will be analyzed to measure student learning outcomes annually. Fellow Focus Groups and mid-year feedback surveys will provide insights into ways the overall project might be improved to better facilitate implementation for future cohorts and maximize student learning. Faculty leaders will use CRIT and Faculty feedback to: (1) make updates to the workshop and overall program design, (2) suggest improvements to the TA and Sway platforms, and (3) augment the set of onboarding and implementation resources available to existing and future cohorts.	  
In addition, a rich layer of automated analytic data from Sway will provide quantitative evaluation of student progress on civil discourse. For each structured dialogue (for example, on immigration, or transgender athletes), Sway generates machine-coded measures of a student’s argumentative clarity, responsiveness to counterarguments, fairness of representation, and degree of belief updating. These indicators allow statistical analyses at scale. For example, Sway can compute nonparametric statistics such as Wilcoxon signed-rank tests and Hodges–Lehmann estimators to quantify improvement across paired observations—for instance: Wilcoxon signed-rank: W \= 2, p \= 1.000; Hodges–Lehmann Δ \= 0.50 (95% CI: –0.58 to 1.58).  
These outputs demonstrate not only whether improvement occurred, but the magnitude and uncertainty of that improvement, using metrics appropriate for data that are ordinal, non-normal, or heterogeneously distributed—common in natural-language reasoning tasks. Sway’s automated analytics therefore give the evaluator immediate access to effect sizes, confidence intervals, and distributional characteristics for tens of thousands of dialogues, enabling more sensitive detection of meaningful gains than instructor-graded samples alone.

# Sample Lesson: Declaration Independence

[**How to read the *Declaration of Independence* as an Argumentative Essay**](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1djkl1q8QB1SKs5U1IVmnCRlxWBwdJrHn/edit?usp=drive_link&ouid=112677525031402621318&rtpof=true&sd=true)

 

**Gary Comstock**

**A lesson in the series, thinkARGUMENTS, ESSAYS: Foundations of American Democracy**

 

 

**Assignment**

1\.  	Read the [Declaration of Independence](https://www.archives.gov/founding-docs/declaration-transcript).

2\.  	Read the [two essays](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1djkl1q8QB1SKs5U1IVmnCRlxWBwdJrHn/edit?usp=drive_link&ouid=112677525031402621318&rtpof=true&sd=true) below. Each takes an opposing view about the Declaration’s argument. The first, “Universalist,” interpretation argues that the document is a philosophical charter arguing for the equality of all human beings. The second, “Originalist,” interpretation argues that the document is a historically situated announcement of America’s grievances against Great Britain.

3\.  	As you read, watch for four elements of each essay: the author’s main claim, their reasons for it, objections that can be raised against it, and ways the author could rebut those objections. Finally, decide for yourself which argument is the strongest.
