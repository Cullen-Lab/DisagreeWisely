Foundation classification: 509(a)(2) explained

The Form 1023 selects 509(a)(2) — a public charity that normally receives more than one-third of its support from contributions, membership fees, and gross receipts from exempt activities, and not more than one-third from gross investment income and net unrelated business income.

This is appropriate for DW's funding profile: primarily grants (contributions) plus anticipated program service revenue. The alternative — 509(a)(1) — requires support primarily from government or the general public, which is less fitting since DW's revenue comes from specific foundations and grants.

This classification should be noted in the board reference for the record.

Background: What are the OFAC follow-up questions (10a–10c)?

If Q10 is changed to “Yes,” three follow-up questions apply. All are straightforward compliance commitments:

  • 10a (OFAC SDN List check): Will you check the OFAC “Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons” list before doing business with anyone abroad? This is a US Treasury list of sanctioned individuals, organizations, and entities. The answer is “Yes” — you’d describe your practice (e.g., “we vet partner institutions against the OFAC SDN list before entering into agreements”).
  • 10b (Sanctions compliance): Will you comply with all US sanctions laws, executive orders, and regulations administered by OFAC? Straightforward “Yes.”
  • 10c (OFAC licensing): If you ever need to operate in a sanctioned country (e.g., providing educational services in Iran or North Korea), will you get the required OFAC license first? “Yes.”

These are boilerplate compliance questions. For a nonprofit deploying an educational platform at universities, the answers are Yes/Yes/Yes and Foundation Group would handle the standard language.

Note  Lebanon: why it's low-risk despite Tier 3

Lebanon is the only country on the list where an IRS reviewer might pause. But the risk is effectively zero for DW, for three reasons:

  1. The institution matters more than the country. The Lebanese American University (LAU) is a US-affiliated institution — it was founded in 1835 as a Presbyterian missionary school, operates under a New York State charter, is accredited by NEASC (a US regional accreditor), and has deep ties to American higher education. It is not an OFAC-listed entity.
  2. DW’s activity is educational program deployment, not fund transfers. DW is not sending money to Lebanon or contracting with Lebanese entities. It is making an educational platform available to a US-chartered university that happens to operate in Lebanon.
  3. OFAC’s Lebanon-related designations target Hezbollah-affiliated entities — banks, shell companies, and individuals linked to the organization. A US-chartered university is the opposite of an OFAC concern.

If Q10 is changed to “Yes” and Lebanon is listed, Foundation Group may want to include a brief note in the description clarifying the nature of the activity (educational platform access at a US-affiliated university). But this is a matter of thoroughness, not concern.

Would disclosing foreign operations complicate the application?

No. Answering “Yes” to Q10 triggers three OFAC compliance questions (10a–10c), all of which are boilerplate “Yes” answers. It also requires naming the countries and briefly describing operations. For a US-based nonprofit deploying an educational platform at well-known universities in allied countries, this is routine.

The risk of not disclosing is arguably greater: if the IRS discovers during review that DW is already operating in 8 countries (e.g., from the activity narrative, grant applications, or website), an undisclosed “No” answer could raise questions about the completeness of the application. Proactive disclosure of straightforward international activity is better than having the IRS find it on their own.
