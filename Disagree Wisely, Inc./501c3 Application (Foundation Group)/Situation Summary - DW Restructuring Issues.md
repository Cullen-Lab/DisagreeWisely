## 5. The Core Problem

**The 501(c)(3) application narrative says DW is cleanly separated from Sway IP development. The operational and financial reality does not yet fully match this narrative.**

Specifically:

1. **The 2026 HxA agreement** (active, signed) explicitly commits DW to "Execute technical development, testing, deployment, and evaluation of the Sway platform." This directly contradicts the restructuring.

2. **DW is currently paying development contractors** via the DW card (Simon's Upwork account). These transactions appear in DW's bank records.

3. **DW is currently invoicing HxA** for development-related reimbursements. These invoices document DW's development activities.

4. **The resulting IP belongs to Simon and Nick personally.** So DW is using its organizational capacity (legal entity, bank accounts, contractor relationships) to facilitate development of IP owned by its officers.

5. **DW received $150K from the Learning Agency specifically for development,** spent ~$25K on development contractors, and still holds ~$125K of development-earmarked funds that need to be returned.

6. **The AVDF grant description is publicly available** and explicitly mentions "mobile app development and UI/UX enhancements." If the IRS traces the connection from DW → HxA → AVDF, they would see DW participating in a project that includes IP development.

7. **The branding clause** requires DW to display "Developed in collaboration with Heterodox Academy" on swaybeta.ai for 10 years. **However**, this language is ambiguous and does not necessarily imply IP development. HxA's actual role under the agreement is community building, campus partnerships, evaluation support, and dissemination — i.e., helping develop the *initiative*, not the software. The agreement's own IP clause (Section 4) explicitly states all software IP belongs to Simon and Nick, precluding the reading that HxA (or DW on HxA's behalf) produced IP. **This clause likely does not need to be changed** and may even serve as a defense — it shows HxA's role was about the broader initiative, not code.

8. **The for-profit entity doesn't exist yet.** There is currently no alternative entity to take over the development activities.

### Why This Matters for the IRS

The IRS's primary concerns for 501(c)(3) applications are:
- **Private benefit/inurement:** Is the organization being used to benefit private individuals (especially insiders)?
- **Exempt purpose:** Are the organization's activities genuinely charitable/educational?
- **Commercial activity:** Is the organization operating as a for-profit business?

A nonprofit that pays for development of software personally owned by its officers — even if reimbursed — raises private benefit concerns. The IRS could view DW as facilitating IP development for the personal benefit of its two key officers, regardless of the reimbursement.

4. **HxA is a nonprofit, not a for-profit.** The HxA arrangement is between two nonprofits, not between a nonprofit and a commercial entity. This reduces (but does not eliminate) concerns.

## 7. Risk Assessment: Not Volunteering Specific Details

Given Ren's advice to be "purposefully vague" about specific tools and companies, a possible strategy is to fix all operational issues behind the scenes while not proactively mentioning the Learning Agency development funds or the HxA/AVDF arrangement in the application. This section assesses the risks of that approach.

### What the application would show:
- Aggregate financial numbers that are accurate (the budget Ren just reviewed)
- Activity descriptions that accurately reflect DW's current and planned activities (research, education)
- No specific mention of Learning Agency development grant, HxA services agreements, or AVDF

### Risks:

**IRS follow-up questions (MEDIUM).** The IRS routinely sends follow-up letters. They might ask: "List your major donors," "Describe all contracts," "Explain the professional fees," or "Describe relationships between the org and its officers." If the behind-the-scenes fixes are done, these can all be answered truthfully and consistently with the application narrative. If fixes are NOT done, truthful answers would contradict the narrative.

**IRS finds the AVDF grant description (LOW-MEDIUM).** It's publicly available and names Sway and mentions "mobile app development." If an IRS reviewer Googles the organization or its founders, they could find it. But reviewers process thousands of applications — thorough investigation isn't guaranteed. If found, the restructuring story is the defense.

**The swaybeta.ai branding clause (LOW).** "Developed in collaboration with Heterodox Academy" appears on the Sway website. However, "developed" is ambiguous — HxA's role per the agreement is community building, campus expansion, evaluation, and dissemination (developing the *initiative*), not producing IP. The agreement's own IP clause explicitly says all software IP belongs to Simon and Nick. This clause likely reads as benign and may even serve as a defense showing HxA's non-development role.

**Future audit (LOW but consequential).** Even if the initial application succeeds, the IRS can audit later. If they discover undisclosed development activities, it could be grounds for revocation. But if DW has genuinely restructured and the fixes are real, the story is defensible.

**Bank records (LOW unless audited).** DW's bank statements show HxA payments, Upwork transactions, and the Learning Agency deposit. Not automatically reviewed during the application, but requestable.

### What makes this strategy VIABLE:

1. **The fixes are actually done** — development payments stopped, HxA agreement amended, LA funds resolved. The strategy is "fix everything AND don't volunteer details." NOT "hide things and hope nobody notices."
2. **Every potential IRS question can be answered truthfully** without contradicting the application.
3. **The aggregate financials are accurate.** Nothing is misrepresented — just presented at the level of detail the template requires.
4. **DW's current activities genuinely are** research and education.

### What makes this strategy RISKY:

1. The fixes **aren't done** by the time of submission.
2. The IRS finds the AVDF description **AND** DW still has a development-focused HxA agreement.
3. A direct question requires an answer that **contradicts** the application narrative.

### Bottom line:

Not volunteering specific details is consistent with Ren's vagueness strategy and is defensible, **provided the operational fixes are thorough and complete.** The two non-negotiable fixes before submission are:
1. Amend the HxA agreement (so any contract the IRS might see is clean)
2. Stop development payments through DW (so current financials are clean)
