About Disagree Wisely

▾

Disagree Wisely, Inc. is a Florida nonprofit corporation incorporated on April 2, 2025. We are a civil discourse research and education organization: we investigate how people can learn to reason together across genuine differences, and we develop and deliver evidence-based educational programs grounded in that research.

The organization grew out of work at Carnegie Mellon University, where co-founders Simon Cullen and Nicholas DiBella developed educational tools — including Sway, an AI-scaffolded dialogue platform — for structured cross-perspective discussion in university classrooms. Disagree Wisely was originally incorporated with the intent of housing both the Sway platform and the surrounding research program. After extensive consultation with Foundation Group (our 501(c)(3) advisors), the organization restructured in early 2026 to draw a clean separation: Sway’s intellectual property remains with the co-founders, who plan to establish a separate for-profit Public Benefit Corporation (PBC) to hold it, while Disagree Wisely focuses exclusively on research, education, and program delivery — using a variety of educational tools in its work.

Mission Statement

We aim to empower millions of learners to cultivate essential intellectual virtues — humility, cognitive empathy, critical thinking, and the ability to engage productively across differences. Driven by rigorous empirical research, we deploy and study innovative educational tools that foster free inquiry and intelligent discussion.

How Email Voting Works

▾

The board frequently acts by unanimous written consent by email rather than holding a meeting. Here is how the process typically works:

  • Initiation: Simon or Nicholas emails all directors with the text of a proposed resolution and a request for your vote.
  • Review: Read the resolution. If you have questions or concerns, reply to ask — there is no deadline pressure until you are comfortable.
  • Voting: Reply-all with your vote (e.g., “I vote in favor”) so all directors can see it. Your email signature serves as your signed consent under the Bylaws (§ 4.7 and § 8.4).
  • Unanimity required: Written consent in lieu of a meeting requires all directors to vote in favor. If any director objects or abstains, the matter must go to a meeting instead.
  • Record-keeping: The email thread is preserved as the official record of the board action.

Your Fiduciary Duties

▾

As a director of a nonprofit corporation, you have three fundamental fiduciary duties:

  • Duty of CareAct with the care an ordinarily prudent person would exercise in a similar position. In practice: attend meetings, read materials before voting, and ask informed questions.
  • Duty of LoyaltyPut the organization’s interests above your own. Disclose any conflicts of interest and recuse yourself when appropriate. (See the Conflict of Interest Policy.)
  • Duty of ObedienceEnsure the organization follows the law and operates within its stated charitable mission.

These duties are the legal foundation for everything else in this guide — the governance policies, financial controls, and insurance protections all exist to support you in fulfilling them.

Conflict of Interest Policy

▾

Adopted July 29, 2025. Signed by Cullen, DiBella, and Thomason.

Annual attestation: Per Foundation Group, the board must attest to understanding this policy annually, and that attestation should be recorded in meeting minutes. The IRS looks for this if they audit. Individual signed acknowledgment forms are not required for the IRS application, but the board may choose to use them as a best practice.

This policy is especially important because two directors (Simon Cullen and Nicholas DiBella) also serve as paid officers. Key provisions:

  • Disclosure duty: If you have a direct or indirect financial interest in any transaction the organization is considering, you must disclose all material facts to the board.
  • Determining a conflict: After your disclosure, you leave the room (or call). The remaining directors decide whether a conflict exists.
  • Recusal: If a conflict is found, you may present information but must then leave while the disinterested directors deliberate and vote.
  • Compensation votes: A director who receives compensation from the organization may not vote on matters pertaining to their own compensation. They may provide information to a compensation committee.
  • Board composition: The majority of the board must be non-salaried and not related to salaried personnel.
  • Annual attestation: The board attests to understanding this policy annually, recorded in meeting minutes.
  • Periodic reviews: The board must periodically review whether compensation arrangements are reasonable, using appropriate comparability data.

What This Means for You

▾

Can I be personally sued for board decisions?

In theory, yes — anyone can file a lawsuit. In practice, you have strong protections. The D&O policy covers up to $1M for claims arising from “wrongful acts” in your capacity as a director, and your legal defense is paid on top of that $1M (defense costs are outside the limit). If the organization cannot indemnify you, you pay $0 deductible — the insurer covers your defense from dollar one. The bylaws separately eliminate personal monetary liability unless you engaged in self-dealing, willful misconduct, or recklessness. You would need a final court judgment establishing fraud or illegal conduct before any policy exclusion could remove coverage — until then, the insurer pays for your defense.

Am I responsible for things that happened before I joined?

Your fiduciary duties begin when you join the board — you are not retroactively liable for past decisions you had no part in. The D&O policy has no retroactive date limitation, so it covers claims arising from acts going back to June 2024 (when the co-founders’ work began), even if those acts preceded your tenure.

What if the organization can’t pay its debts?

Directors are not personally liable for organizational debts. Disagree Wisely is a separate legal entity (a Florida nonprofit corporation). The D&O policy explicitly continues through bankruptcy or insolvency of the organization, and the policy’s payment priority ensures that non-indemnifiable claims — where you as an individual are personally on the hook — are paid first, before entity-level claims.

What should I do if I receive a legal demand or become aware of a potential claim?

Notify Nicholas DiBella or Simon Cullen immediately. The D&O policy requires written notice to the insurer within 60 days of a claim. Do not admit liability, enter into any settlement, or agree to any judgment without the insurer’s prior written consent. The insurer has the right and duty to defend covered claims and selects defense counsel. For D&O claims, report to Berkley Select Claims Department: newclaim@berkleyselect.com or (844) 465-6256. For cyber incidents, call the CFC 24-hour hotline: 1-844-677-4155 or email cyberclaims@cfc.com.

What kinds of claims are not covered by the D&O policy?

The main exclusions are: intellectual property disputes (plagiarism, copyright, patent); antitrust violations; professional services claims; bodily injury or property damage; and employment practices claims (discrimination, wrongful termination). Claims involving criminal conduct or illegal personal profit are excluded only after a final court adjudication — defense is provided in the meantime. Many of the risks not covered by the D&O policy (professional services, IP, data breaches) are separately covered by the Cyber/Tech policy.

Does coverage continue after I leave the board?

Yes. The D&O policy covers claims against past directors for wrongful acts during their tenure. The bylaws’ indemnification rights also continue after you leave and extend to your heirs and estate. Additionally, if the policy is not renewed, there is an automatic 60-day extended reporting period (and an option to purchase up to 3 years of additional reporting time).

March 6, 2026 — First Full Board Meeting (2026 Annual Meeting)

▾
Meeting Details

Organization: Disagree Wisely, Inc.

Date: March 6, 2026

Time: 4:00 PM – ~5:46 PM Eastern

Location: Zoom videoconference

Type: First Full Board Meeting; also serves as the 2026 Annual Meeting (Bylaws § 4.1)

Minutes prepared by: Nicholas DiBella, Secretary

Directors Present

  • Dr. Simon Cullen — President & CEO
  • Dr. Nicholas DiBella — Secretary, Treasurer & CTOO
  • Dr. Neil Thomason — Director
  • Ken Stern — Director
  • Dr. Barbara Oakley — Director

Quorum confirmed: 5 of 5 directors present (3 required).

Recording Consent

The Secretary (Nicholas DiBella) asked whether all directors consented to recording the meeting for the purpose of producing accurate minutes, with the recording to be destroyed after the minutes are finalized. He noted the recording would be transcribed locally (not uploaded to the cloud) and destroyed after minutes are produced. All five directors consented. Recording was started.

1. Welcome & Introductions

The President welcomed Ken Stern and Barbara Oakley as new directors. Brief introductions were made for the record. The President noted that this meeting also serves as the 2026 Annual Meeting under Bylaws § 4.1 (delayed from January to accommodate onboarding of the two new directors). He confirmed quorum: all five directors present.

The President set expectations that a written onboarding guide and comprehensive reference site would be sent after the meeting, covering everything discussed in more detail.

2. Annual Governance Items

Officer Re-Elections (Bylaws § 5.1):

The President explained that the Bylaws require annual officer elections. Each officer abstained from the vote on their own re-election. The following elections were held:

PositionCandidateResultVotes
PresidentSimon CullenApproved4-0 (Cullen abstained)
Secretary & TreasurerNicholas DiBellaApproved4-0 (DiBella abstained)
DirectorNeil ThomasonApproved4-0 (Thomason abstained)

For each vote, the four non-abstaining directors (the remaining directors) voted yay. All three re-elections were unanimous among voting members.

Conflict of Interest Attestation: The President noted that the COI policy document would be sent to all directors after the meeting. Each director is to reply by email attesting that they have read and understand the policy and will comply. This serves as the annual attestation required by the policy and expected by the IRS.

Bylaws Receipt: The President noted that the Bylaws would be sent to Ken Stern and Barbara Oakley after the meeting for email confirmation of receipt and agreement to abide by them.

3. 501(c)(3) & Restructuring Update

Restructuring: The President explained the structural separation between Disagree Wisely (nonprofit: research, education, program delivery) and Sway IP (to be held by a separate for-profit Public Benefit Corporation). This separation was recommended by Foundation Group to avoid private benefit concerns with the IRS. The PBC has not yet been incorporated but can be done for approximately $50 in Delaware.

501(c)(3) Application: Foundation Group has everything they need and is now producing the official application for submission in the coming weeks. Correction from prepared slides: the President stated that expedited IRS review will not be requested (revising the earlier plan). The application will go through standard review.

HxA/AVDF Compliance Transition: The current Phase 2 services agreement (Jan–Nov 2026) includes development language that predates the restructuring. The Secretary reported that DW had proposed an amendment to remove development costs from DW’s scope, but HxA Executive Director Michael Regnier informed them the previous day that this is not feasible with approximately three months remaining in the grant — it would require legal counsel review, HxA board COI approval, and AVDF approval. The President elaborated that the practical outcome is that DW will continue invoicing HxA for some development-related costs through May/June 2026, when the remaining AVDF development budget is expected to be used up. The net financial cost to DW is zero (fully reimbursed).

4. Learning Agency / Tools Competition Funds (~$125K)

Simon and Nick won the Learning Agency Tools Competition Growth Prize ($150K) for Sway. $25K was spent on Instructor Insights as proposed. ~$125K remains earmarked for ChatMaps (a real-time argument visualization feature). Because DW is restructuring away from development, these funds cannot be compliantly spent on ChatMaps through DW.

The President reported that the original hope of returning the ~$125K to the Learning Agency for redirection to the PBC is not possible — the funds come from a bucket restricted to nonprofits. However, the Learning Agency is open to restructuring the award so that the funds can be used for other compliant purposes within DW (operational and scaling costs, research, infrastructure). The Learning Agency responded with “no concerns on our end regarding reallocation and the adjustments to your plan.” The President will meet with them again to finalize the restructured agreement and report back to the board. Financial impact: the ~$125K is currently included in DW’s gross cash (~$173K).

5. Sway IP & CMU Dispute

The President briefed the board on the CMU IP dispute. Carnegie Mellon has asserted a joint ownership interest in the Sway platform IP. The co-founders’ position is that Sway qualifies as educational courseware under CMU’s own IP policy (Section 3-6-1), which vests ownership in the creator “in all cases.” CMU-affiliated code contributions amount to 0.77% of the codebase (454 lines of commodity CSS, HTML, and boilerplate). Previous counsel (David Kalson, Dentons) assessed the legal position as strong. The Dentons engagement ended in February 2026.

Board input:

  • Ken Stern recommended obtaining independent legal counsel (not Foundation Group) and offered to connect Simon with Laura Ellsworth at Jones Day as potential counsel.
  • Ken noted both IP law and employment law dimensions to the dispute.
  • The board discussed the option of removing the 0.77% CMU-affiliated code entirely.

6. Financial Report

The Treasurer (Nicholas DiBella) presented the financial overview:

  • Gross cash on hand: ~$173,000 (includes ~$125K Learning Agency funds being retained pending reallocation)
  • Operating costs: ~$40K–$45K/month to maintain current pace of development and pay the core team
  • No one is currently paid directly from DW’s general funds. Simon Cullen is paid by HxA. Nicholas DiBella’s salary is funded by the Craig Snider Foundation grant, with funds transferred to HxA so he remains on HxA’s payroll. Rachel Sipser (Director of Communications) will be paid through HxA soon (back pay owed).
  • Board members (Thomason, Stern, Oakley) are uncompensated for board service. Simon and Nick also have separate operational roles (CEO and CTOO) for which they are compensated.
  • Secured funding awaiting 501(c)(3): FIPSE ~$800K (Year 1 ramp-up: ~$60K to DW; larger amounts in subsequent years; funds disbursed yearly to Notre Dame)
  • Revenue sources reviewed: HxA/AVDF (~$284K across two phases), Craig Snider Foundation ($50K), Lindy Snider Foundation ($25K), Ben Delo Foundation ($20K remainder), HxA Sway Classroom grant ($13,171), and the Learning Agency Tools Competition ($150K). Pending applications: ECI/Wake Forest (~$233K), NewSchools Venture Fund ($100K–$200K), Templeton World Charity Foundation ($5M+ if invited to full application).

7. Growth & Impact

The President reported on organizational growth:

  • Sway has been used by ~10,000 students and ~130 instructors across 80+ institutions (cumulative)
  • Up from ~30 institutions / ~2,000 students at the start of 2025
  • University-level adoption discussions underway with University of Michigan (potentially 8,000 students in orientation), SUNY system, Columbia University, CUNY, and MIT

8. Open Discussion & Board Member Input

Ken Stern offered:

  • Help with University of Michigan contacts, CUNY (friends with the chancellor), and SUNY contacts
  • Introduction to Laura Ellsworth (Jones Day) for the CMU IP matter

Barbara Oakley offered:

  • Oakland University connections, including a conference on social discourse

9. Next Steps

Several post-meeting follow-up items were identified, including sending governance documents for attestation, distributing onboarding materials, and follow-up actions assigned to individual directors. See the Action Items section for the complete list with owners and status.

Adjournment

There being no further business, the meeting was adjourned at approximately 5:46 PM Eastern.
