# Privacy Policy Language Review — Sway / Disagree Wisely

**Date:** March 21, 2026
**Scope:** Review of the Sway privacy policy (https://web.swaybeta.ai/privacy-policy.html) for consistency with the Form 1023 (501(c)(3) application) and the organizational restructuring that separates Disagree Wisely (nonprofit research and education) from Sway IP (personally owned by co-founders, eventually to be held by a for-profit Public Benefit Corporation).

---

## The Problem

The Sway privacy policy opens with:

> "Disagree Wisely, Inc. ("we," "us," or "our") is a Florida nonprofit corporation (501(c)(3) pending) that **operates** Sway (SwayBeta.ai)..."

The Form 1023 (501(c)(3) application) describes Disagree Wisely's relationship to educational tools as:

> "The organization may **license** research tools or educational software from third-party entities for use in research studies, at no cost or a reduced rate."
>
> "These tools are **used solely as instruments** within research studies evaluating methods of classroom discussion and civil discourse."
>
> "The organization **does not develop or own intellectual property** related to those tools."
>
> "The organization does not promote, endorse, or commercially distribute these tools, and **retains full independence** over research design, analysis, and publication of results."

These two descriptions are inconsistent. The Form 1023 frames DW as a research and education organization that licenses third-party tools. The privacy policy frames DW as the operator of a software platform. If the IRS reads the publicly accessible privacy policy, they may ask why the nonprofit that "does not develop or own intellectual property related to those tools" describes itself as operating one.

---

## What the Form 1023 Says DW Does and Does Not Do

**DW does:**
- Conduct scientific research and educational programs
- License third-party tools for use in research studies
- Use those tools as instruments within its research
- Retain ownership of research outputs (publications, validated instruments, findings)
- Provide educational programming (conferences, workshops)

**DW does not:**
- Develop or own IP related to the tools it uses
- Operate, promote, endorse, or commercially distribute those tools
- Give any individual preferential access to research results

---

## Language Spectrum: From Most to Least Consistent with Form 1023

| Language | Consistent? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "DW **uses** educational tools **in its research and educational programs**" | Yes | Matches Form 1023's "instruments within research studies" |
| "DW **conducts research and educational programs** using Sway" | Yes | Positions Sway as a tool within DW's programs |
| "Instructors and students **use** Sway **as part of** DW's programs" | Yes | Passive; Sway is just a tool |
| "DW **provides access to** educational programs" | Mostly | Slightly more active but doesn't claim to operate a platform |
| "DW **deploys** educational programs" | Borderline | "Deploy" implies DW is rolling something out; OK for DW's own programs, not for describing its relationship to Sway specifically |
| "DW **deploys Sway**" | Inconsistent | Implies DW is the entity putting Sway into the field; blurs the line between DW's programs and the third-party tool |
| "DW **operates Sway**" | Inconsistent | Directly contradicts Form 1023's framing of tools as licensed third-party instruments |

### Key distinction

The Form 1023 draws a line between:
- **DW's own programs** (research studies, conferences, workshops) — DW conducts these
- **Tools used within those programs** (e.g., Sway) — DW licenses these from third parties

Language that describes DW conducting its own programs is fine. Language that describes DW operating, deploying, or running specific third-party tools blurs this line.

## Recommended Privacy Policy Language

### Current (problematic):

> "Disagree Wisely, Inc. ("we," "us," or "our") is a Florida nonprofit corporation (501(c)(3) pending) that operates Sway (SwayBeta.ai), an educational discussion platform that connects college and university students in AI-facilitated peer conversations. This Privacy Policy explains how we collect, use, store, and protect information from all users of our platform and website, including students, instructors, and website visitors."

### Proposed (consistent with Form 1023):

> "Disagree Wisely, Inc. ("we," "us," or "our") is a Florida nonprofit corporation (501(c)(3) pending) that conducts research and educational programs focused on constructive disagreement and civil discourse. As part of these programs, instructors and students use Sway (SwayBeta.ai), an educational discussion platform that connects college and university students in AI-facilitated peer conversations. This Privacy Policy explains how we collect, use, store, and protect information from all users of Sway and our website, including students, instructors, and website visitors."

### What changed:
- **"operates Sway" → "conducts research and educational programs"** — consistent with Form 1023
- **Sway introduced as something used within those programs**, not something DW operates
- **"our platform" → "Sway"** — avoids possessive language implying DW owns the platform

### What stayed the same:
- DW is still the "we" that collects and manages data — this is a practical necessity because DW is currently the only legal entity involved (the PBC does not yet exist), and a privacy policy must name a responsible data controller

## The Data Controller Question

A privacy policy must name a responsible entity — the data controller — who is accountable for how user data is collected, processed, and stored. Right now, that is DW, because:

1. The PBC does not yet exist
2. DW's officers (Simon and Nick) are the ones who personally own the Sway IP and manage all operations
3. There is no other legal entity to point to

This is an inherent tension in the transition period. DW needs to be named as the responsible entity in the privacy policy, but the Form 1023 says DW merely licenses third-party tools.

**The resolution:** DW can be the data controller for its research and educational programs (which is what the Form 1023 describes) without being the operator of the platform itself. The framing matters: "We collect data as part of our research and educational programs" is different from "We collect data because we operate this platform."

---

## Should the PBC Be Created First?

**No — not for the purpose of fixing the privacy policy.** The privacy policy language can be fixed now with the reframing described above. Creating the PBC to solve the privacy policy problem would introduce more issues than it resolves:

1. **A shell PBC doesn't help.** Incorporating a PBC in Delaware takes an hour and $50, but putting a brand-new shell entity's name on a privacy policy governing 10,000+ students' data would be worse than the current situation. The "operator" of the platform would be a company with no employees, no bank account, no EIN, no insurance, and no operational capacity. That's not credible to regulators, institutions, or users.

2. **The CMU IP dispute is unresolved.** The board specifically discussed getting independent legal counsel (Laura Ellsworth, Jones Day) before taking actions on the IP. Transferring IP to a PBC while CMU claims joint ownership could complicate the dispute or create new legal issues. Legal counsel should weigh in before the PBC is created.

3. **Timing with the 501(c)(3) application.** Creating a related-party PBC owned by DW's officers while the Form 1023 is under IRS review could trigger questions. The Form 1023 describes the PBC as a future plan — creating it during the review period and immediately entering into a licensing arrangement with it could prompt the IRS to scrutinize the relationship more closely. Better to let the application process complete or at least be well underway.

4. **The PBC needs to be real, not just incorporated.** For the PBC to credibly be the data controller and platform operator, it would need: an EIN, a bank account, insurance (at minimum cyber/tech liability), a formal license agreement with DW, data processing agreements, employees or contractors, and server infrastructure in its name. This is not a one-day task.

**The right sequence:**
1. **Now:** Fix the privacy policy language to be consistent with the Form 1023 (reframe DW as conducting programs that use Sway — no PBC needed)
2. **Soon:** Get legal counsel (Laura Ellsworth) on the IP situation and PBC structure
3. **When legal picture is clear:** Incorporate the PBC
4. **When PBC is operational:** Transition the privacy policy to the PBC as the data controller and Sway operator

The privacy policy fix and the PBC incorporation are independent workstreams. Don't couple them.

---

## When the PBC Is Created

Once the PBC is incorporated and holds the Sway IP:

1. **The privacy policy should transition to the PBC** as the entity that operates Sway and controls platform data
2. **DW should be described as a research partner** that accesses de-identified data through a formal data-sharing agreement with the PBC
3. **A data-sharing MOU between DW and the PBC** should be executed, specifying:
   - What data DW can access (de-identified research data)
   - For what purposes (research and education only)
   - Retention and deletion terms
   - That DW's access is independent of the personal relationships between the co-founders (i.e., the agreement survives changes in leadership)
4. **The Form 1023's framing becomes fully accurate** — DW licenses a third-party tool (from the PBC) and uses it as an instrument in its research

## General Guidance on Language Going Forward

### When describing DW's relationship to Sway (or any educational tool):

**Use:**
- "conducts research and educational programs"
- "uses [tool] in its research studies"
- "licenses [tool] from [entity]"
- "provides educational programming that utilizes [tool]"
- "instructors and students use [tool] as part of the organization's programs"

**Avoid:**
- "operates [tool]"
- "runs [tool]"
- "our platform" / "our tool" (when referring to Sway or similar)
- "develops [tool]" (unless referring to materials DW itself creates, like survey instruments)
- "deploys [tool]" (borderline; OK for DW's own programs, not for describing relationship to third-party tools)

### When describing DW's relationship to research data:

**Use:**
- "retains ownership of research outputs (publications, instruments, findings)"
- "uses de-identified data for research purposes"
- "collects data as part of its research and educational programs"

**Avoid:**
- "owns the data" (DW owns research outputs, not raw platform data)
- "our data" (when referring to raw platform/user data)

### When describing DW's own programs:

**Use freely:**
- "conducts," "runs," "provides," "delivers," "offers"
- "DW's research program," "DW's educational programs," "DW's conferences"

These are DW's own programs and DW can describe them with active, operational language. The restriction applies only to describing DW's relationship to third-party tools and IP it does not own.

---

## Summary

The privacy policy's "operates Sway" language is inconsistent with the Form 1023. The fix is straightforward: reframe DW as conducting programs that use Sway, rather than as the operator of Sway. This is not just a semantic distinction — it reflects the legal and structural separation between DW (nonprofit research/education) and the Sway IP (personally owned, eventually PBC) that Foundation Group specifically designed to avoid private benefit concerns with the IRS.

All public-facing language about DW's relationship to Sway should follow the Form 1023's framing: DW licenses and uses third-party educational tools as instruments in its research and educational programs.

## Addendum: Simon's Response and Revised Assessment (March 22, 2026)

Simon reviewed this analysis and responded:

> "But is operation not exactly what DW does when it licenses and uses Sway IP to make it available to schools? I think Claude might be being paranoid/hyper cautious."

**Simon has a fair point.** This analysis was probably too cautious on several items. Here's a revised assessment separating what's real from what's overcorrected:

### Where Simon is right

DW does, in practice, operate Sway. It runs the servers, manages accounts, handles support, pays for infrastructure (GCP, Anthropic, OpenAI, Twilio, etc.). You can operate something you don't own the IP for — a franchise operates a restaurant without owning the brand. "Operates" is a description of what DW does operationally, not an IP ownership claim.

The privacy policy needs to tell users who is responsible for their data, and that entity is DW. Saying DW "operates" Sway is the most natural and accurate way to convey that to users.

### Where the concern was real, but conditional

This privacy policy review was done against the Form 1023 *as written at the time*. The Form 1023's Research Safeguards section says DW uses tools "solely as instruments within research studies" and "does not promote, endorse, or commercially distribute these tools." Against that specific language, "operates" is inconsistent.

However, we've since flagged that exact Form 1023 language with Foundation Group (Ren) as potentially too restrictive — it may not accurately reflect what DW actually does (educational deployments, cost recovery from university partners, active recruitment of institutions). If Ren softens the Form 1023 language to match DW's actual operations, the tension between the Form 1023 and the privacy policy largely disappears.

### Revised recommendation

**The privacy policy isn't the primary problem — the Form 1023 language is.** The right sequence:

1. **Wait for Ren's response** on the Research Safeguards language (the "solely," "promote/distribute," and "full independence" questions)
2. **See how the Form 1023 evolves** based on that conversation
3. **Then revisit** whether the privacy policy needs changes — and if so, align it with the updated Form 1023

If the Form 1023 is updated to accurately describe DW's operational role (deploying educational tools at universities, receiving compensation for support, collaborating on research design), then "operates" in the privacy policy is fine.

### Changes that are still worth making regardless

A few items from the original analysis remain valid independent of the Form 1023 language:

- **"our AI Guide" / "our rephrase suggester"** → "the AI Guide" / "the rephrase suggester" — these are Sway features, and possessive language implies DW owns them. This is an IP framing issue, not an "operates" issue.
- **"improve the product"** → "improve the educational experience" — "the product" implies DW develops/owns it.
- **"our servers"** → "the servers" — DW doesn't own the servers (they're on GCP).

These are minor and can be done whenever the privacy policy is next updated.
